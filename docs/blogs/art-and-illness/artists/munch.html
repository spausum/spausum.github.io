Edvard Munch

Edvard Munch was born in 1863 and experienced several traumatic experiences in his childhood. His mother and sister died from tuberculosis before he turned 15. His younger sister was diagnosed with mental illness and his brother died as a young man. His father was unsupportive of his pursuit of the art, preferring Munch to have studied engineering. These experiences helped shape his artistic expression.


Munch struggled with alcoholism, depression, and suicidal thoughts for many years. After having a breakdown, he checked himself into a clinic. There, his doctor encouraged him to continue painting as part of his recovery.


He used his art as a form of therapy, exploring themes of love, death, and anxiety. He often used his own experiences to express this symbolism. One of his most famous works is The Scream, which he describes as "the expression of all suffering of mankind." Another, The Sick Child, is depicted on the left. This painting depicts a mother holding the hands of a sick child. The painting could be connected to Munch's childhood when he experiences the death of his sister to tuberculosis.


Edvard Munch's art reflects his struggles. He used art as a way to express and heal himself.
